Metric Weather is a responsive web application designed to help water sports enthusiasts maximize their time on the water.

The aim of the app is to provide the exact forecast that every user wants. Users have the ability to edit the metrics that appear in their forecast so that they see the information they want, and none that they don't.

My Process

The very first thing I did to determine the direction Metric Weather should go was conduct a competitive analysis. This would help me determine what the existing market was like to find the gap that my app could fill.

Competitive Analysis

I conducted a competitive UX analysis on two existing weather apps, one general marine weather app and one sport specific app, to more fully understand the market.

General Marine Weather App

This app's goal is the provide accurate wave, wind, and weather forecasts along with easy easy to read charts. They emphasize their "professionally designed UI/UX" and user friendly app.

  • Accurate forecasts
  • Provides essential forecast data without any walls of text or numbers
  • Ability to search specific stations or recommend new ones

Strengths

  • General information covers lots of needs
  • Clean UI makes information easy to read

Weaknesses

  • Lacks in-depth details for professionals
  • Some big features are behind a paywall

Opportunities

  • UI has room for aesthetic and accessability improvement
  • App has no flexibility to match user needs

Threats

  • Many apps have more sport specific information
  • All information is available on website

Sport Specific App

This app's goal is make it easy for surfers to find out what the conditions are at locations near them so they can decide where and when to surf.

  • Accurate forecasts and live updates for almost 3,000 locations in 180 countries
  • Forecast charts in addition to reports made by real surfers
  • Ability to access live web-cams to view the locations you are reading about

Strengths

  • Covers all needed surf information
  • Visually easy to read
  • Dedicated user base

Weaknesses

  • Design has room for improvement
  • "Less information" option in app removes very little

Opportunities

  • Is not available on iPad
  • Can offer longer term forecast free (instead of subscription)

Threats

  • Other large competitors offer the same services
  • Poor reviews may lose them users

From my analysis I made two discoveries:

  1. General weather apps lack the details or flexibility needed to give experts a complete weather forecast
  2. Sport specific apps had a very large amount of information but no way to limit the amount of information you can see

From these discoveries I realized that I wanted Metric Weather to be a flexible app that could fit the needs of beginners and experts, as well as single sport or multi-sport athletes.

Problem Statement

After conducting a competitive analysis, interviews with water sports enthusiasts, and hypothesis testing this is what I came up with:

The Problem:

Existing water sport forecast apps specialize in one sport or in providing general forecasts that lack metrics experts need. Sport beginners can be overwhelmed with data, while sport experts may desire extra information an app cannot provide. Both of these can lead to dangerous mistakes that affect safety.

The Solution:

Water sport enthusiasts need a way to accurately gauge water and weather statistics based on their personal skill level. We will know this to be true when less than 5% of users who engage with this app report that the weather differ from our forecast.

User Research Findings

I conducted interviews with three water sports enthusiasts to understand their behaviors, needs, and pain points. After an affinity map and analysis here are my findings.

Findings that supportet my problem statement:

  • Weather metrics needed varies by sport and by experience level
  • Most interviewees use more than one app/website to get data
  • Interviewees trust what they can see and know their own limits. Experience level changes needs
  • Interviewees want to maximize their time on the water and get the most out of that time

Findings that led to user persona 1 (Jeff):

  • Some users trust their own eyes over any application or advisory
  • Some users go to a different locations all over the world for their sport
  • Some users like to plan trips just to do a sport

Findings that led to user persona 2 (Sabrina):

  • Some users trust advisories and applications over their own judgement
  • Some users can only do their sport from one main location
  • Some users can only do their sport sporadically

Test Results:

Looking at the card sort data shows that while water sport locations are always grouped together, and specific water sports are always grouped together, the metrics of weather are further divided. The weather metrics are split into water related weather (water temp, wave height, et.c), air related weather (air temp, cloud cover, et.c), and wind weather (wind speed and direction.) Since my initial hypothesis was that every different water sport athlete needs different weather statistics, it makes sense that the weather metrics were grouped together and not with any location or sport. My initial sitemap had four second level pages for sailing, diving, surfing, and location. But based on the way the card sort revealed that locations and sports are so inherently connected, I decided to move all of the individual sport pages under a main sport page, and create sub-pages under locations.

Site Map Revision

This new site map has a critical first step in determining the type of information a user would be looking for: either sport specific info, or location specific info. From there, the user will have more targeted information that they can then edit to craft the perfect weather report they are looking for.

Usability Test Results

From my usability test I was able to identify a number of errors and observations that helped me see what was lacking in my first iteration. The largest error was that I had completely omitted a screen in the desktop version that was available in the mobile app. The test also showed me what features users felt were essential to the app. I had planned for some features to be added later on, but after this round of testing I realized users expected it immediately.

Final Version

My preference test was intended for me to pick a login screen to use for Metric Weather. One option focused on a tagline informing users about the app. The other utilized images of locations and sports related to water. While the option with images had the majority of votes, many testers expressed that they liked the tagline so I decided to combine both elements in the final version.
